FFT & FIR Filter Design Case Studies

1. Frequency Analysis of Noisy ECG Signal

Scenario: Electrocardiogram (ECG) signals are often contaminated by noise such as powerline interference, baseline wander, and muscle artifacts. To extract meaningful information, frequency analysis and filtering techniques are required.

Problem Statement: You are given a noisy ECG signal that can be approximated as:

ECG(t) = A1*sin(2πf1t) + A2*sin(2πf2t) + ... + Noise

Signal and Noise Components:

  • ECG Fundamental Frequencies: f1 = 1 Hz, f2 = 5 Hz, f3 = 10 Hz
  • Amplitudes: A1 = 1, A2 = 0.8, A3 = 0.5
  • Powerline Interference: 50 Hz (Amplitude = 0.3)
  • Baseline Wander: 0.1 - 0.5 Hz (Amplitude = 0.4)
  • Muscle Noise: 20 - 300 Hz (random high-frequency noise)
  • Sampling Rate: 500 Hz
  • Duration: 5 seconds

Tasks:

  • (a) Plot the noisy ECG signal using MATLAB or Python.
  • (b) Design a bandpass filter (0.5 Hz - 100 Hz) to remove noise components.
  • (c) Compute the 8-point Radix-2 FFT and analyze frequency components.
  • (d) Visualize and compare the frequency spectrum before and after filtering.
  • (e) Discuss the impact of filtering on signal quality.

Hint: Start by plotting the raw ECG signal with noise before applying filtering and FFT.

2. Low-Pass FIR Filter Design for Speech Processing

Scenario: Speech signals require filtering to remove high-frequency noise and retain intelligibility. A low-pass FIR filter is designed to enhance the quality of speech recordings.

Problem Statement: You are given a sampled speech signal with a maximum frequency of interest at 4 kHz. Your task is to design a low-pass FIR filter to preserve the speech components while eliminating unwanted higher frequencies.

Filter Specifications:

  • Cutoff Frequency: 4 kHz
  • Sampling Frequency: 16 kHz
  • Filter Order: 50
  • Window Function: Hamming

Tasks:

  • (a) Design and implement the FIR filter using MATLAB or Python.
  • (b) Compare the frequency response of Hamming and Blackman windows.
  • (c) Analyze the impact of windowing on filter performance.
  • (d) Apply the designed filter to a speech sample and evaluate the results.

Hint: Compare Hamming and Blackman windows by plotting their magnitude responses.

3. FFT-Based Circular Convolution for Audio Filtering

Scenario: You need to apply fast convolution using FFT to filter an audio signal. This approach is widely used in real-time applications where computational efficiency is crucial.

Given Data:

  • Sampling Rate: 8 kHz
  • Frame Size: 16 samples
  • Low-pass FIR Filter: 7 coefficients
  • Impulse Response: h = [0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8, 0.6, 0.4, 0.2]
  • Input Signal: x = [1, 2, 3, 4, 3, 2, 1, 0, -1, -2, -3, -4, -3, -2, -1, 0]

Tasks:

  • (a) Perform circular convolution manually using the given data.
  • (b) Implement FFT-based convolution in MATLAB or Python.
  • (c) Compare the results and discuss computational benefits of FFT-based convolution.

Hint: Use FFT to transform both signals, multiply them, then apply IFFT to get the result.

4. Resampling and Frequency Response of Digital Filters

Scenario: You need to downsample a signal and analyze how the frequency response changes after resampling.

Given Data:

  • Analog Filter: Low-pass filter with a cutoff at 7 kHz
  • Original Sampling Rate: 48 kHz
  • Target Sampling Rate: 16 kHz
  • Downsampling Factor: 3
  • Signal Frequency Components: 500 Hz, 3 kHz, 8 kHz, 12 kHz
  • Anti-Aliasing Filter: Low-pass FIR filter with 7 kHz cutoff
  • Filter Order: 9 (Hamming window)

Tasks:

  • (a) Design and apply the anti-aliasing filter before downsampling.
  • (b) Perform downsampling by a factor of 3.
  • (c) Compute and visualize the frequency response before and after downsampling for N=9 samples.
  • (d) Discuss the effects of aliasing in the downsampled signal.

Hint: Use FFT to analyze the frequency spectrum before and after downsampling.
